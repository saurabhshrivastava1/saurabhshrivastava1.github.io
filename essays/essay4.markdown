    <h2 style="font-style: italic; margin-bottom: 5px;">RPA to Agentic AI</h2>
    <p style="color: #888; font-size: 12px; margin-bottom: 30px;">January 2026</p>

    <p> How Great Leaders Navigate a Shifting Tech Landscape by Asking Better Questions </p> 
   
    <p> Technology never stands still. Every few years, a new wave of innovation crashes over the business world, reshaping what’s possible, redefining how companies operate, and raising the stakes for leaders navigating these tides. From Robotic Process Automation (RPA) to traditional AI, from generative AI to the emerging world of agentic AI, each evolution brings both promise and peril. </p>
   
    <p> In such a fast-moving, high-stakes environment, what remains constant? The ability of great leaders to ask the right questions. Not the trend-chasing, buzzword-laden ones but clear, fundamental, business-grounded questions that cut through the noise and guide responsible, value-driven decision-making. </p> 

    <p> <b> Why Questions Matter More Than Ever </b> </p> 
    <p> Over the years, I’ve seen while working on everything from back-office RPA implementations to enterprise AI forecasting systems, that no matter how advanced the technology gets, good decisions still come from better questions. They might sound simple: 


    - What problem are we truly solving for our customers?
    - How will we measure success?
    - What could go wrong and how do we mitigate it?
    - Are our actions aligned with our company’s long-term strategy and ethical commitments?
    - But simple doesn’t mean easy. And in the whirlwind of emerging technologies, leaders who consistently ask these kinds of questions position their businesses not just to survive but to thrive.

    Let’s walk through how each phase of AI evolution has raised new and increasingly complex questions for business leaders to wrestle with. </p>

    <p> <b> RPA: Automating the Repetitive </b> </p>  
    <p> When Robotic Process Automation arrived, it promised to free human teams from tedious, repetitive tasks. The formula was clear: the higher the volume and frequency of rule-based workflow, the better the case for automation. Bots worked tirelessly with speed, accuracy, and consistency. </p>
    
    <p> The leadership questions then were operational and tactical:

    - Which processes should we automate?
    - Is it technically feasible, economically viable, and beneficial to customer experience?
    - How do we scale while maintaining quality and compliance?
    - These were implementation-focused, but laid the groundwork for the larger AI conversations to come. </p>

    <p> <b> Machine Learning: From Rules to Predictions </b> </p>
    <p> As machine learning matured, businesses moved beyond rules-based systems to predictive models that could anticipate demand, detect fraud, or personalize recommendations. </p>

     <p> The questions evolved too:
    - How reliable is our data? Is it clean, complete, and free from bias?
    - How frequently should models be updated, and how do they integrate with business workflows?
    - What’s our tolerance for errors like false positives or overfitting? </p>

    <p> Leaders quickly learned that in predictive systems, outputs are only as good as the data and assumptions feeding them. </p>

    <p> <b> Generative AI and LLMs: Creating and Conversing </b> </p>
    <p> The arrival of generative AI and large language models (LLMs) fundamentally shifted what AI could do. Now, systems could write, converse, and create - transforming marketing, customer service, software development, and beyond. </p>

     <p> With that power came a new set of strategic, ethical, and operational questions:

    - What tangible business value will this deliver — in revenue growth, efficiency, or customer experience?
    - What’s our long-term platform and data strategy as AI capabilities evolve?
    - How do we manage risks around hallucination, misinformation, bias, and security?
    - How do we uphold our company’s ethical standards while experimenting with these powerful tools? </p>

    <p> Generative AI wasn’t just a technical tool, it became a cultural, strategic, and operational force businesses had to reckon with. </p>



    <p> <b> Agentic AI: Autonomous Decision-Making </b> </p>
    <p> Today, we stand on the edge of the next frontier : agentic AI. These systems don’t just follow rules, make predictions, or generate content; they act. They make decisions autonomously, learn over time, and in some cases, negotiate and execute complex, multi-step tasks on behalf of users. </p>

     <p> And once again, the questions leaders must ask grow in depth and consequence:

    - Where can agentic AI genuinely differentiate our business - improving margins,  customer satisfaction, or operational resilience?
    - What are the technical, ethical, and societal risks in deploying AI agents at scale?
    - How do we build guardrails, governance, and accountability into systems making autonomous decisions?
    - And most critically, how do we ensure these systems consistently reflect our values, purpose, and responsibility to customers and society?

    Because with great autonomy comes great accountability. </p>


    <p> <b> Bottom Line: The Constant Amid Change </b> </p>
    <p> Technology will continue to evolve. RPA, machine learning, generative AI, agentic AI,  and whatever comes next - will reshape the landscape in ways we can’t fully predict. </p>

     <p> But what would not change is this: the leaders and businesses that endure aren’t those chasing the latest tech trend. They’re the ones grounded in clear thinking, disciplined decision-making, and a relentless commitment to asking better, sharper, values-driven questions. </p>
    
    <p> Because in an era where the tools are faster, smarter, and more autonomous than ever before, it’s the human judgment behind the questions that will always matter the most. </p>
